# Addison, TX — LVT Legal Brief

**Date of analysis:** 2026-06-11
**Status:** Blocked at all vehicles under current law. Constitutional amendment required before any LVT-equivalent reform is implementable.
**Recommended vehicle:** Vehicle B (universal building exemption) via constitutional amendment to Tex. Const. Art. VIII §2 — the lower-bar of the two Tier 7 routes.
**Pathway tier:** 7 (state constitutional amendment)
**Confidence:** High on the block. Medium on the precise Vehicle B amendment route vs. Vehicle A route — both require amendment; which is more politically accessible is a political judgment.

---

## 1. Jurisdictional snapshot

- **State:** Texas
- **City:** Town of Addison — home rule municipality, incorporated 1953, home rule charter adopted. Population ~16,000. Dallas County.
- **Taxing authorities in scope:** City of Addison only (this brief covers the city levy; Dallas County, DISD, and other overlapping levies are outside scope).
- **FY2025 city levy rate:** $0.6081 per $100 of appraised value.
- **FY2025 ACFR total levy:** $39.5M (real property ~$33.4M + BPP ~$5.9M).
- **Home rule status:** Home rule, but property tax structure is bound by Texas Constitution Art. VIII regardless of charter authority. No Texas home rule city has or can have differential millage on land vs. improvements under current constitutional text.

## 2. Constitutional layer

### Uniformity clause

Texas Constitution Art. VIII §1(a): **"Taxation shall be equal and uniform."**

Texas Constitution Art. VIII §1(b): **"All real property and tangible personal property in this State, unless exempt as required or permitted by this Constitution, shall be taxed in proportion to its value, which shall be ascertained as may be provided by law."**

*Source: [Tex. Const. Art. VIII §1](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/CN/htm/CN.8/CN.8.1.htm); also cited in [Justia Texas Constitution Art. VIII](https://law.justia.com/constitution/texas/articles/cn000800.html).*

This is the **TX-type** pattern identified in the decisioning guide (§2): "equal and uniform" combined with "in proportion to its value" is the strictest uniformity formulation among U.S. states. The clause:

1. Mandates equal treatment of all taxable real property ("equal and uniform").
2. Mandates the tax be proportional to appraised market value ("in proportion to its value").
3. Locates exemption authority exclusively in the constitution itself ("unless exempt as **required or permitted by this Constitution**").

Point 3 is the critical constraint on Vehicles B and C: the legislature cannot create new categories of exempt property through ordinary statute. Exemptions must be constitutionally grounded.

### Classification and exemption authority

**Classification authority:** Texas Constitution Art. VIII contains no grant to the legislature to classify real property into land vs. improvements (or any other sub-classification) for differential rate purposes. This is in direct contrast to Maryland Declaration of Rights Art. 15, which expressly authorizes separate land/improvement assessment and classification.

**Exemption authority:** Art. VIII §2 enumerates constitutionally mandated or permitted exemptions. These include: (a) public property (federal, state, county, public institutions); (b) religious, charitable, and educational institutions (non-profit); (c) family homestead of a surviving spouse (with legislature permitted to expand); and (d) agricultural and open-space land under use-value appraisal. The legislature may also create additional exemptions **only if the constitution authorizes it explicitly** — either within Art. VIII §2 or via a new amendment.

**Agricultural use-value precedent:** Texas Constitution Art. VIII §1-d and §1-d-1 authorize the legislature to provide for appraisal of agricultural, timber, and open-space land at productive-use value rather than market value. This is the constitutional model for a building exemption: a new §1-d-2 (or analogous provision) authorizing the legislature to exempt improvements on land from taxation would be structurally parallel to the agricultural carve-out and could serve as Vehicle B's constitutional hook.

**Critical distinction:** The agricultural carve-out lowers assessed *value* for a carve-out class; a building exemption would exempt an asset class entirely (or partially). Both require the same constitutional mechanism — enumerated authorization in Art. VIII.

### Constitutional rate caps

Texas has **no aggregate rate cap** analogous to California's 1% (Prop 13) or Washington's 1% limit. The fiscal constraint on property tax rates is the voter-approval rate mechanism in statute (see §5 below), not a constitutional ceiling. This is favorable: it means a future LVT rate structure would not run into a constitutional rate ceiling.

### Voted-levy carve-outs

Texas Constitution Art. VIII does not create a voted-levy escape from the uniformity clause. A voter-approved rate above the statutory voter-approval ceiling remains subject to "equal and uniform" and "in proportion to value." Voter approval authorizes a higher amount of tax, not a different *structure* of tax.

### Key judicial interpretations

No reported Texas Supreme Court case has directly addressed the question of whether a universal building exemption would violate Art. VIII §1(a)+(b). The court has interpreted the uniformity clause strictly in analogous contexts. *Dallas County Appraisal District v. Les Moulins de la Martinique, Inc.* (cited in property tax treatises) and related cases establish that uniformity applies parcel-by-parcel across the class of taxable real property. The agricultural use-value carve-outs (§1-d, §1-d-1) have survived constitutional challenge precisely because they are **constitutionally enumerated** exceptions, not legislative creations. A legislatively-created building exemption without a constitutional hook would face the same challenge and likely fail.

## 3. Statutory layer

### Property Tax Code

The Texas Property Tax Code (Tex. Tax Code Title 1) is built on a single-base, uniform-rate framework. Key provisions:

- **Tex. Tax Code §23.01:** "All taxable property is appraised at its market value as of January 1." No provision for separate appraisal *rates* on land vs. improvements — they are appraised separately but taxed at a single uniform rate.
- **Tex. Tax Code §26.04:** Tax rate-setting framework. A taxing unit sets one rate applied to all taxable value. No provision for bifurcated rates.
- **Tex. Tax Code §26.07:** Voter-approval elections. Allows voters to approve rates above the no-new-revenue ceiling. Does not modify the uniformity requirement.
- **Tex. Tax Code Ch. 11:** Statutory exemptions. Implements constitutionally authorized exemptions (homestead, disability, veterans, agricultural use-value, etc.). Does **not** include a general building exemption — none is constitutionally authorized to create.

### Senate Bill 2 (2019) — rate growth caps

HB 3 / SB 2 (86th Legislature, 2019) replaced the "rollback rate" with the "voter-approval rate" — generally the no-new-revenue rate plus 3.5% for most taxing entities (8% for smaller entities and during declared disasters). A Addison city council wishing to raise the LVT rate above the no-new-revenue baseline would need voter approval via petition election if the increase exceeds 3.5%. This is a procedural constraint on rate increases, not on rate structure.

*Source: [Tex. Tax Code §26.04](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/TX/htm/TX.26.htm); SB 2, 86th Leg., R.S. (2019).*

### No LVT or split-rate enabling statute

Texas has no enabling statute for split-rate property taxation, universal building exemption, or land/improvement classification for differential rates. Searches of Texas Legislature Online for the 87th (2021), 88th (2023), and current 89th (2025) sessions reveal no such pending bills as of the date of this brief. The 88th Legislature's property tax focus was on rate compression for school districts and expanding homestead exemptions (HB 2, 2023), not structural reform.

### Pending legislation

**No pending LVT, split-rate, or building exemption bills identified** in the Texas Legislature as of June 2026. The closest analog — homestead exemption expansion — was addressed legislatively through HB 2 (88th Leg., 2023), which increased the residence homestead exemption from $40,000 to $100,000 under Tex. Tax Code §11.13. This is a value exemption, not a structural reform.

## 4. Local authority

### Home rule status

Addison is a home rule municipality under **Tex. Local Gov't Code §51.001 et seq.** A Texas home rule city "may adopt or amend its charter to provide for its own municipal organization and governance." However, **§51.003 explicitly provides that home rule authority is subject to the Texas Constitution and general state laws.** Home rule does not extend to property tax structure.

*Source: [Tex. Local Gov't Code §51.001](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/LG/htm/LG.51.htm).*

No provision in Addison's municipal charter grants or could grant authority to set different millage rates on land vs. improvements — such authority does not exist in Texas law for any municipality, regardless of charter.

### Prior LVT / classification reform history

No Texas municipality has implemented a split-rate property tax, universal building exemption, or any LVT-equivalent reform. No prior attempts in Dallas or Addison are documented in the public record. The closest Texas has come to structural property tax reform is the agricultural and open-space use-value appraisal system (§1-d, §1-d-1), which dates to constitutional amendments in 1966 and 1978 respectively.

## 5. Caps, limits, and circuit breakers

| Mechanism | Amount | Applies to voted levies? |
|---|---|---|
| Voter-approval rate ceiling | NNR + 3.5% for cities (NNR + 8% small/emergency) | Voted ratification required *above* ceiling, not a hard cap |
| No aggregate % rate cap | None (unlike CA 1%, WA 1%) | N/A |
| Acquisition-value lock | None (unlike CA Prop 13, MI Headlee) | N/A |
| Circuit breaker credit | None at city level | N/A |

**Key finding:** Texas has no constitutional aggregate rate cap and no acquisition-value lock. These are the two most common barriers in other states. This means: (1) once legal authority for LVT exists, rate calibration is unconstrained by any constitutional cap; (2) land values in the model are current market values (not acquisition-locked), so the LVT base is economically meaningful.

The voter-approval rate (SB 2) is a procedural gate on rate increases, not a ceiling. A city that wanted to shift to LVT — once constitutionally authorized — could do so within the no-new-revenue framework (revenue-neutral LVT requires no rate *increase*, only restructuring), potentially without triggering a voter approval election.

## 6. Assessment foundation

Texas has one of the best assessment foundations in the country for LVT purposes:

- **Separate valuation:** DCAD (Dallas Central Appraisal District) appraises land and improvement values separately at the parcel level, annually. These are available in the public parcel file.
- **Full market value:** Texas appraises at 100% of market value as of January 1 each year. No assessment ratio (unlike Ohio's 35%, Minnesota's Tax Capacity). No acquisition-value lock (unlike California's Prop 13 or Michigan's Proposal A).
- **Annual reassessment:** DCAD reassesses annually; values for any given fiscal year reflect the prior January 1 market conditions.
- **Public data:** DCAD publishes per-parcel land and improvement values (LNDVALUE, IMPVALUE) through its ArcGIS MapServer. LVTShift's Addison model uses this data directly.

**Assessment conclusion:** Layer 5 is fully ready. If the legal authority were created tomorrow, Dallas County could administer an LVT immediately using existing appraisal infrastructure — no assessment overhaul needed. This distinguishes Texas from California (Tier 8) and Michigan (Tier 8) and puts it squarely at Tier 7 with no additional assessment work required.

The only minor gap: DCAD's MapServer does not expose homestead exemption flags at the parcel level, so the LVTShift model approximates homestead exemptions by class code proxy. A full implementation would use DCAD's certified tax rolls (available through the county) which include per-parcel exemption flags.

## 7. Vehicles open / blocked / unlock-able

| Vehicle | Status | Primary block | Action to unlock |
|---|---|---|---|
| A. Direct split-rate | **Blocked** | Tex. Const. Art. VIII §1(a)+(b): "equal and uniform" + "in proportion to its value" bars different millage rates on land vs. improvements | Constitutional amendment to Art. VIII §1 adding classification authority (parallel to MD Declaration of Rights Art. 15) |
| B. Universal building exemption | **Blocked (Unlock-able at Tier 7)** | Art. VIII §1(b): exemptions must be "required or permitted by this Constitution" — no current constitutional hook for a universal building exemption | Constitutional amendment to Art. VIII §2 adding building improvements to permitted exempt categories (parallel to §1-d agricultural carve-out) |
| C. Partial improvement exemption / abatement | **Blocked (Unlock-able at Tier 7)** | Same as B; a partial exemption not grounded in Art. | D. Classification shift within existing classes | **Blocked** | Texas has no residential/commercial classification framework with differential rates; all real property is one taxable class | Requires either a constitutional amendment to create classification authority, or repurposing of use-value provisions (§1-d) which are value-based, not rate-based |
| E. Voted-levy LVT | **Blocked** | Voter approval under SB 2 (§26.07) authorizes higher rates, not different rate structures; uniformity clause applies to voted levies equally | Constitutional amendment required; voter approval mechanism is a procedural gate on levy amount, not a structural carve-out |
| F. Tax-increment / value capture (TIF) | **Open** (Tier 1) | No barrier under current law | Tex. Tax Code Ch. 311 authorizes Tax Increment Reinvestment Zones (TIRZs); Addison can create or expand TIRZs by city council ordinance |

**Tier 7 is the operative tier for any LVT-equivalent reform in Addison and Texas broadly.**

Unlike California or Michigan (Tier 8), Texas does not require an assessment-system overhaul alongside the constitutional amendment. Dallas CAD's annual full-market-value appraisals are immediately usable. This is a meaningful advantage — the political and administrative work is limited to the constitutional change, not a parallel technical rebuild.

## 9. Recommended pathway and legal chain of thought

### Recommended vehicle and tier

**Vehicle B (universal building exemption) via Tier 7 constitutional amendment to Tex. Const. Art. VIII §2.** This is marginally more politically accessible than a Vehicle A amendment for two reasons:

1. **Framing:** A building exemption can be presented as "exempting buildings from taxation" — a positive grant to property owners, analogous to the homestead exemption expansion (HB 2, 2023) that passed with broad bipartisan support. A direct split-rate amendment requires explaining rate differentiation, which is harder to communicate.
2. **Constitutional mechanism:** Adding a new category to Art. VIII §2 (exemptions) is structurally parallel to the agricultural use-value amendments (§1-d, §1-d-1) that Texas has passed before. The agricultural-use precedent demonstrates the mechanism works and the voters have approved analogous carve-outs.

A partial improvement exemption (Vehicle C) could be the intermediate step — e.g., an amendment authorizing a 75% building exemption — reducing political resistance while still producing substantial land value incentivization.

### Legal chain of thought

1. **Tex. Const. Art. VIII §1(a):** "Taxation shall be equal and uniform." → All real property must be taxed at the same effective rate, applied uniformly.
2. **Art. VIII §1(b):** "All real property … shall be taxed in proportion to its value, unless exempt as required or **permitted by this Constitution**." → Exemptions must be constitutionally grounded; the legislature cannot create them by statute alone.
3. **Art. VIII §2 (current):** Lists constitutionally mandated/permitted exemptions. Building improvements on land are not currently listed. → No exemption authority for improvements under current constitutional text.
4. **Block:** Therefore, any ordinance, statute, or referendum creating different effective tax rates on land vs. improvements — whether via split-rate (Vehicle A), building exemption (Vehicle B), or abatement (Vehicle C) — violates Art. VIII §1(a)+(b) without a new constitutional provision.
5. **Unlock:** A constitutional amendment adding to Art. VIII §2 (or as a new §1-d-2): *"The legislature may by general law provide that improvements on real property shall be exempt from ad valorem taxation, in whole or in part, subject to such conditions and uniform application as the legislature may prescribe."* — This language (a) authorizes the exemption via the same mechanism as agricultural use-value; (b) preserves "uniform application" within the class of taxable property; (c) gives the legislature flexibility to set the exemption percentage (100% = pure LVT, 75% = Spokane-style abatement).
6. **Post-amendment path:** Once the constitutional amendment passes, the legislature enacts an enabling statute (one session); cities opt in by ordinance (no voter approval required if revenue-neutral).
7. **Addison implementation:** Revenue-neutral restructuring requires no increase above the current levy, so it would not trigger the voter-approval rate election under SB 2. Addison's existing DCAD land/improvement appraisal data is immediately usable.

### Sequence of actions

1. **Advocate at the legislature:** Texas constitutional amendments require a 2/3 vote of both chambers (Tex. Const. Art. XVII §1). This is the controlling political gate. The amendment must be placed on a statewide ballot.
2. **Statewide voter approval:** A simple majority of Texas voters approves the amendment. Ballot language matters enormously — "exempt buildings from property taxes" is more accessible than "permit differential ad valorem rates on land and improvements."
3. **Enabling statute:** Legislature passes a statute authorizing municipalities to apply the building exemption (single session, simple majority after the amendment passes).
4. **City council ordinance:** Addison city council adopts the exemption by ordinance. Revenue-neutral calibration (as modeled in `cities/addison/model.ipynb`) shows median residential tax change of −2.4% and vacant land increase of +143.9% under a 4:1 equivalent.
5. **DCAD implementation:** Dallas CAD continues annual full-market-value appraisals unchanged; the city simply sets a zero (or reduced) millage against IMPVALUE and a higher millage against LNDVALUE.

### Fallback vehicles

In order of next-best option:
1. **Vehicle C (partial improvement exemption)** at Tier 7 — same mechanism as B but politically easier (e.g., 50% improvement exemption as a first-step amendment). Achieves partial LVT economics without the full political lift of 100% building exemption.
2. **Vehicle F (TIF/TIRZ) at Tier 1** — not LVT but available today; captures incremental land value from public investments without changing the underlying rate structure.
3. **Vehicle G (Public Improvement District) at Tier 1** — also available today; can impose benefit-based special assessments on commercial corridors, partly capturing land value increment.

## 10. Creative pathways considered

### Vehicle B through statutory route only (no amendment)

**Considered and rejected.** One could argue that Art. VIII §1(b)'s phrase "unless exempt … as permitted by this Constitution" includes any exemption the legislature provides, since Art. VIII §2 grants broad legislative exemption authority. However, the Texas Supreme Court's strict interpretation of the uniformity clause, combined with the pattern that all existing exemptions are specifically enumerated in the constitution, makes a universal building exemption via legislation very unlikely to survive judicial challenge. The safest and most durable path is the constitutional amendment. **Specific text defeating this reading:** "unless exempt as required or permitted by **this Constitution**" — the constitution itself is the source of authority, not the legislature acting independently.

### Vehicle E as a pure land-value voted levy

**Considered and rejected.** One could structure a new city service funded exclusively by a land-value levy, presented to voters under SB 2's voter-approval election mechanism. But the levy would still need to be applied uniformly to all taxable property under Art. VIII §1. A levy applied only to land value (with buildings exempt from that specific levy) has the same constitutional problem as Vehicle B — the building exemption is not currently permitted. Voter approval under §26.07 is authorization of an *amount*, not a structural carve-out from uniformity.

### Agricultural §1-d-1 "open space" extension to urban land

**Considered and rejected.** Texas Constitution Art. VIII §1-d-1 permits open-space land to be appraised at use-value rather than market value. In theory, the legislature could broadly interpret "open space" to include urban commercial land, producing low appraised values (and thus low taxes) on underdeveloped parcels. But: (a) this is the opposite of LVT — it *reduces* taxes on underdeveloped land; (b) it requires the owner to apply for and maintain special valuation status; (c) it addresses assessment, not rate structure. This pathway achieves the wrong incentive direction.

### Vehicle G (Municipal Management District) layered levy

**Unresolved.** Texas Local Government Code Ch. 375 authorizes Municipal Management Districts with authority to levy assessments. These districts can impose charges based on "a benefit derived from the improvement or service." If a district's benefit methodology could be tied to land value (e.g., proximity to infrastructure), this might approximate LVT economics. However, Ch. 375 assessments are benefit-based and must be proportional to benefit received — not proportional to land value per se. This is a potential creative pathway that requires a Texas-licensed attorney's analysis of whether a land-value-based benefit assessment would survive challenge under Tex. Tax Code §31.01 and related statutes.

## 11. Open questions and required follow-up research

1. **Exact Art. VIII §2 text:** Verify the full text of Texas Constitution Art. VIII §2 and any subsections granting the legislature exemption authority beyond specifically enumerated categories. If there is a general legislative exemption grant in §2, Vehicle B might be achievable at Tier 5 (state enabling statute alone) rather than Tier 7. This is the single highest-value open question.

2. **Texas AG opinion on building exemption constitutionality:** The Texas Attorney General issues formal opinions on constitutional questions (Tex. Gov't Code §402.042). An AG opinion requesting analysis of whether Art. VIII §1(b) permits the legislature to exempt building improvements from taxation without an amendment would be definitively informative. The question has not previously been posed in a reported opinion.

3. **Municipal Management District (Ch. 375) benefit methodology:** Obtain analysis from a Texas property tax attorney on whether a land-value-based benefit assessment in a Ch. 375 district survives challenge. If yes, this is a Tier 1 creative pathway available to Addison today for targeted commercial corridors.

4. **89th Legislature (2025) pending bills:** Full search of Texas Legislature Online for any property tax classification or exemption bills in the current session (2025). As of June 2026, none are known but the session may have produced bills not captured in this analysis.

5. **Addison charter text:** Pull the Town of Addison charter from Municode or eCode360 to confirm whether the charter contains any unusual property tax provisions or anti-preemption language. Unlikely to matter given the constitutional constraint, but worth confirming.

6. **Coalition feasibility for constitutional amendment:** The agricultural §1-d-1 amendment (1978) passed with farmer and rural landowner support. A building exemption amendment would need a different coalition — urban property owners with high improvement values (residential and commercial). Understanding the historical coalition politics of Texas constitutional amendments for property tax is essential before pursuing Tier 7.
